Anatomy of a Reporting Pipeline

This section divider opens the anatomy segment, which runs from roughly minute 25 to minute 38. Say: “Anatomy of a Reporting Pipeline.” Then advance.

This segment breaks down what just happened in the demo into discrete, customizable steps. The goal is to show participants that each step is a lever they can pull — they are not locked into a single output format.

Each Step Is Customizable

Data Steps

  • Query — which systems, which tables, what date range
  • Clean — handle nulls, standardize formats, deduplicate
  • Merge — join across systems on shared keys
  • Compute — KPIs, YoY change, variance to budget

Output Steps

  • Chart — bar, line, waterfall, heatmap; style and palette
  • Narrate — tone, length, audience, what to emphasize
  • Assemble — layout, headers, appendix, export format
  • Review — human-in-the-loop checkpoint before distribution

The pipeline is the same agent loop from Session 3, executed multiple times in sequence. Each step’s output feeds into the next step’s input.

Say: “Let me break down what you just saw into its component parts, because each one of these steps is customizable. That is what makes this powerful — you are not locked into a fixed report format.”

Walk through the data steps: “On the left, the data steps. Query: you control which systems to pull from, which tables, what date range. Clean: handle missing values, standardize formats — remember the Legacy CRM stores dates as text strings? That needs cleaning. Merge: join across systems using shared keys, with fuzzy matching for inconsistent names. Compute: calculate the metrics you care about — year-over-year changes, variance to budget, headcount ratios.”

Walk through the output steps: “On the right, the output steps. Chart: you choose the chart type, the style, the color palette. Narrate: you control the tone — formal for the board, conversational for a team meeting — the length, the audience, what to emphasize. Assemble: layout, headers, whether there is an appendix, the export format. And review: a human-in-the-loop checkpoint before anything gets distributed.”

“The key insight here is that the pipeline is the same agent loop from Session 3, just executed multiple times in sequence. Each step’s output feeds into the next step’s input. The agent queries the data, feeds the results to the merge step, feeds the merged data to the compute step, and so on.”

“And here is where it gets interesting: you can change any single step without changing the others. Want a different chart type? Change the chart instruction. Want a different tone? Change the narrate instruction. Want to add a new data source? Add it to the query step. The pipeline is modular.”

Transition: “Now let me show you how to use that modularity to iterate on quality.”

The Art of Iteration

This section divider opens the iteration demo segment, which runs from roughly minute 38 to minute 43. Say: “The Art of Iteration.” Then advance.

This is a quick five-minute demo showing how prompt specificity drives output quality. You will ask for the same report three times with increasingly specific prompts.

Prompt Specificity Drives Quality

Generic Prompt

  • “Summarize Q3 results”
  • Gets a wall of text
  • No structure, no emphasis
  • Fine for a first draft

Structured Prompt

  • “Use traffic-light format: green/yellow/red for each division”
  • Clear visual hierarchy
  • Easy to scan
  • Much more useful

Executive Prompt

  • “One page, lead with biggest risk, include 90-day action items”
  • Board-ready output
  • Prioritized by impact
  • Actionable recommendations

Say: “The quality of what you get out depends entirely on the specificity of what you put in. Let me show you three levels.”

“Level one: the generic prompt. ‘Summarize Q3 results.’ You will get a wall of text. It will be factually correct, but it will not be useful. No structure, no emphasis, no prioritization. Fine for a first draft, but you would never send it to your board.”

“Level two: the structured prompt. ‘Use traffic-light format — green, yellow, red — for each division.’ Now you have visual hierarchy. You can scan it in 30 seconds and know where the problems are. Much more useful.”

“Level three: the executive prompt. ‘One page maximum. Lead with the biggest risk. End with three recommended actions for the next 90 days.’ Now you have a board-ready document. Prioritized by impact. Actionable.”

“The underlying data is the same in all three cases. The difference is entirely in the prompt.”

DEMO INSTRUCTIONS: Switch to the XYZ app. Do three quick iterations. First: “Summarize quarterly performance.” Show the generic output. Second: “Redo this using traffic-light format with red, yellow, and green for each division. Flag any metric more than 10% off plan.” Show the improvement. Third: “Make this one page maximum. Lead with the biggest risk. End with three recommended actions.” Show the board-ready version. Each iteration takes about 30-60 seconds. Narrate the differences as they appear.

After the demo: “Each iteration took seconds. The cost of refining is essentially zero. The skill is knowing what to ask for — and that is a business skill, not a technical one. You know what your board wants to see. You know what format works. Now you have a tool that can produce it.”

Iteration in Practice

Draft 1: “Summarize quarterly performance.” — You get a generic overview.

Draft 2: “Use traffic-light format. Flag any metric more than 10% off plan.” — Now it’s scannable.

Draft 3: “One page maximum. Lead with the biggest risk. End with three recommended actions.” — Board-ready.

Each iteration takes seconds. The cost of refining is near zero. The skill is knowing what to ask for — and that’s a business skill, not a technical one.

Say: “Here is the pattern in text form. Draft one: generic. Draft two: structured. Draft three: board-ready. Three iterations. Each one took 30 seconds. Total cost: maybe a penny in API fees.”

“The important thing to internalize is that the first draft does not need to be perfect. The first draft needs to be good enough to iterate on. You are not writing the report — you are directing the report. And the vocabulary of direction is format, tone, length, focus, audience, and emphasis.”

“I want you to use this vocabulary in the hands-on exercises we are about to do. Do not accept the first draft. Iterate at least twice.”

From Ad Hoc to Automated

This section divider opens the automation segment, which runs from roughly minute 60 to minute 72. Say: “From Ad Hoc to Automated.” Then advance.

This segment bridges from the hands-on exercises to the production reality. The key insight is that the pipeline architecture is identical whether a human triggers it via chat or a scheduler triggers it via cron job.

Same Pipeline, Different Trigger

Ad Hoc (Chat)

  • User types a request
  • Agent runs pipeline
  • Delivers to the user
  • Great for exploration and one-offs

Automated (Scheduled)

  • Cron job triggers the pipeline
  • Monday morning dashboard refresh
  • Monthly board deck generation
  • Human review gate before distribution

The architecture is identical. The only difference is who triggers it — a person typing in chat or a scheduler running on a timer.

Say: “Everything you just did in the exercises — the customer risk report, the P&L comparison, the board memo — you triggered it by typing in a chat window. But in production, these same pipelines can run on a schedule.”

“On the left: ad hoc. You type a request, the agent runs the pipeline, delivers to you. Great for exploration, one-offs, and answering questions as they come up.”

“On the right: automated. A cron job — a scheduled task — triggers the exact same pipeline. Monday morning at 6 AM, the agent runs the weekly performance review. First of the month, it generates the board deck. Every Friday, it refreshes the sales forecast.”

“The architecture is identical. The same agent loop from Session 3, the same pipeline steps we just walked through. The only difference is who triggers it — a person or a scheduler.”

“But there is one critical addition in the automated version: a human review gate. The report is generated, but it is not distributed automatically. It goes to an approver — a manager, a controller, a VP — who reviews it, makes any needed edits, and then releases it. This is the maker-checker pattern we will discuss in depth in Session 6.”

Ask the room: “Think about your organization for a moment. What reports get produced on a regular schedule? Weekly? Monthly? Quarterly? How many person-hours go into producing them?” Let a few people share. Then say: “Now imagine those reports being drafted automatically, with a human just reviewing and approving. That is what we are talking about.”

Production Pipeline Architecture

1

Scheduler

Cron, Airflow, or cloud scheduler

2

Agent Pipeline

Same loop: query, compute, chart, narrate

3

Human Review

Approver checks output before distribution

4

Distribute

Email, Slack, SharePoint, or dashboard

Walk through the four steps. Say: “Here is the production architecture. Step one: a scheduler kicks off the pipeline. This could be a simple cron job, Apache Airflow if you have a data engineering team, or a cloud scheduler like AWS EventBridge or Azure Logic Apps.”

“Step two: the agent pipeline runs. Same loop we have been looking at all session — query, compute, chart, narrate, assemble. The code is identical to what ran when you typed in the chat window.”

“Step three: human review. This is essential. The output goes to an approver before it goes anywhere else. The approver checks the numbers, verifies the narrative makes sense, and either approves or sends it back for revision. In production, you never distribute an AI-generated report without human review. We will talk more about why in Session 6 when we cover trust and verification.”

“Step four: distribute. Once approved, the report goes out — email, Slack, SharePoint, a dashboard, whatever your organization uses.”

“The beauty of this architecture is that steps two through four are the same regardless of what triggers step one. A person typing in chat, a scheduled job, a webhook from another system — the pipeline does not care. It just runs.”

Transition: “This is not hypothetical. Let me show you a real-world example of exactly this pattern at enterprise scale.”

This section divider opens the workshop segment, which runs from roughly minute 72 to minute 85. Say: “Workshop.” Then advance.

Identify Your Pipelines

Workshop — Your Recurring Reports

  • Identify 2–3 recurring reports at your company that follow this pattern
  • For each, answer:
    1. What data sources does it pull from?
    2. Who prepares it today, and how long does it take?
    3. Who reviews and approves it?
    4. What would “good enough to automate” look like?
  • Be ready to share one example with the class

Say: “For the next 12 to 13 minutes, I want you to think about your own organization. Identify two to three recurring reports that follow the pattern we have been discussing — data from multiple systems, compiled into a deliverable, distributed on a regular schedule.”

“For each report, answer four questions. One: what data sources does it pull from? Two: who prepares it today, and how long does it take? Three: who reviews and approves it before distribution? Four: what would ‘good enough to automate’ look like? Not perfect — good enough. What level of accuracy and polish would you need before you would trust an automated draft?”

“That fourth question is the most important one, and it is the one most organizations skip. They think about whether the technology can produce the report, but they do not think about what the acceptance criteria are. If the automated version is 90% as good and takes 95% less time, is that good enough? For most internal reports, the answer is yes.”

“Take about 8 minutes to work through this individually, and then we will share. I want each person to be ready to describe one of their recurring reports — what it is, where the data comes from, and what ‘good enough to automate’ would mean.”
