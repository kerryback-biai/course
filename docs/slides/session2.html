How Data Moves Today

Four Patterns of Data Integration

APIs

  • Real-time, point-to-point
  • Each connection hand-coded
  • Breaks when vendors change endpoints

iPaaS

  • MuleSoft, Workato, Boomi
  • Pre-built connectors
  • Still needs mapping and maintenance

ETL / Warehouse

  • Extract, transform, load into Snowflake/BigQuery
  • Single source of truth
  • $500K–$5M and 6–18 months

CSV Export

  • The “just email me the file” approach
  • No governance, no audit trail
  • Surprisingly common

The warehouse is the gold standard — but the cost, timeline, and failure rate are why most mid-market companies don’t have one. The alternative: query each system where it lives and merge on the fly.

Two Approaches, Real Tradeoffs

Data Warehouse AI Agent (Query-in-Place)
Setup cost $500K–$5M Low (API credentials + schema docs)
Time to first answer 6–18 months Days to weeks
Data freshness Batch (hourly/daily) Real-time queries
Governance Strong (single schema) Weaker (distributed)
Complex joins Fast (pre-modeled) Slower (runtime merge)
Scalability Excellent Limited by API rate limits
Best for Regulated, high-volume Exploratory, ad hoc
Audit trail Built-in Requires custom logging

This is not either/or. Most companies will use both — warehouses for governance-critical reporting, agents for the long tail of questions that never justified a dashboard.

Meridian has ~25 customers that appear under different names across its three CRMs. This is not a bug — it is the normal state of enterprise data.

The Fuzzy Matching Problem

How Does the Agent Resolve “General Electric” vs. “GE Industrial Solutions”?

  • Exact match fails — the strings are completely different
  • Fuzzy matching (Levenshtein distance, token-set ratio) catches ~80% of cases
  • Semantic matching (embeddings) catches more, but is slower and less transparent
  • Human review is still needed for ambiguous cases (is “Honeywell Safety Products” a subsidiary or a division?)

This is the unsexy core of data integration. No amount of AI eliminates the need to decide what your entities are and maintain a mapping.

Data Map Workshop

Map Your Company’s Data

Step 1: List Your Systems

  • Identify 3–5 core systems (CRM, ERP, HRIS, etc.)
  • What data lives in each?
  • Who owns it?

Step 2: Draw the Gaps

  • What cross-system questions can nobody answer easily?
  • Where do people resort to spreadsheets?
  • What decisions are made on gut feel because the data is too hard to get?

Step 3: Identify Quick Wins

  • Which two systems, if connected, would unlock the most value?
  • What’s the simplest question that currently takes weeks?

What We’re Skipping (For Now)

The Pre-Work Nobody Talks About

Before any AI agent can query your real systems, someone must:

  • Catalog data — what tables exist, what do the columns mean?
  • Document schemas — data types, relationships, business rules
  • Provision API access — authentication, rate limits, permissions
  • Classify PII — which fields contain personal data? What are the retention rules?

Key Takeaway

The hardest part isn’t technology.

It’s that your systems disagree.

They disagree on who your customers are, what your products are called, and when things happened. AI can bridge the gap — but someone still has to define the truth.
