The Key Principle

Final Draft, Not First Draft

Don’t review AI’s first draft. Instead:

  1. Ask AI to produce the work
  2. Ask it to critique itself and revise
  3. Repeat until no further revisions are needed
  4. Now review what it produced

You review a final draft instead of a first draft. This saves your time (though it burns tokens). The more consequential the work, the more revision cycles it deserves.

Here is the single most important principle for working with AI. Do not review the first draft. That is wasting your time on the worst version of the output. Instead, ask the AI to produce the work, then ask it to critique itself and revise, and repeat until the revisions stop improving. Only then do you review. You are reviewing a final draft instead of a first draft. This saves enormous amounts of your time. The AI’s time is cheap — a few cents per revision. Your time is expensive. Let the AI iterate on itself before you invest your attention.

The Iteration Cycle

📄 Draft Get the first version — fast but mediocre

🎯 Direct Give specific feedback, not “make it better”

🔍 Self-Critique Ask Claude to find its own weaknesses

✨ Fix Apply the critique — dramatically better output

Here is the cycle. Step one: get a first draft. It will be fast but usually mediocre — too generic, too long, missing nuance. Step two: give specific direction. Not “make it better” but “make it specific to our Q4 results” or “the chart is misleading — use a zero baseline” or “rewrite the executive summary in half the length.” Step three — and this is the power move — ask Claude to critique its own work. “What is weak about this analysis? What would a skeptical CFO push back on?” Step four: tell it to fix those issues. Two rounds of self-critique produce dramatically better output than any amount of vague “improve this” prompting.

The Secret: Specificity

Vague (Weak)

  • “Make it better”
  • “Improve the analysis”
  • “Make it more professional”
  • “Fix the chart”
  • “Add more detail”

Specific (Strong)

  • “Lead with the biggest risk to Q4 revenue”
  • “Add year-over-year comparisons for each metric”
  • “Cut the background section and start with the recommendation”
  • “Use a zero baseline and add data labels”
  • “Include the three customers that drove the variance”

Here is the core principle. Vague feedback produces vague improvement. Specific feedback produces specific improvement. When you say “make it better,” Claude has to guess what you mean. When you say “lead with the biggest risk to Q4 revenue,” it knows exactly what to do. This is true of managing people too, by the way — but it is even more true of managing AI, because AI has no context about your preferences, your audience, or your organizational culture unless you tell it explicitly.

The Maker/Checker Framework

Now let me formalize something we previewed at the end of Session 1. The maker/checker framework. This is how you think about every AI output for the rest of this course — and for the rest of your career using these tools.

The Core Shift

The professional skill is shifting:

From producing analysis → To directing and verifying it

AI is the maker. You are the checker.

The fundamental shift is this: the professional skill used to be producing analysis. Building the spreadsheet, writing the report, creating the presentation. Now the skill is directing and verifying. You tell the AI what to make, and then you check whether it made it correctly. This is not a lesser skill — it requires deep domain expertise, judgment, and an understanding of what “correct” means in context. But it is a different skill, and developing it is what this course is about.

Calibrated Trust

🟢 Low Stakes

  • Internal notes
  • Brainstorming sessions
  • Personal exploration
  • First-draft ideas

Verification: Quick sanity check. Glance at axes, skim for obvious errors.

🟡 Medium Stakes

  • Team presentations
  • Internal reports
  • Meeting summaries
  • Analysis for your manager

Verification: Verify every number. Check that framing matches reality. Test key claims.

🔴 High Stakes

  • Board presentations
  • Regulatory filings
  • Public-facing documents
  • Financial disclosures

Verification: Independent verification of every claim. Cross-check sources. Second reviewer.

Calibrated trust means matching your verification effort to the consequences of being wrong. Low stakes: internal notes, brainstorming, personal explorations. If the chart has a minor error, nobody is hurt. A quick sanity check is sufficient. Medium stakes: team presentations, internal reports, analysis for your manager. If the numbers are wrong, your credibility takes a hit. Verify every number. Check that the framing matches what you know to be true. High stakes: board presentations, regulatory filings, anything public-facing. If these are wrong, the consequences are severe. Every claim needs independent verification. You should cross-check sources and have a second reviewer. The right answer is not “always check everything” — that defeats the purpose of using AI. The right answer is “check proportionally to what happens if it is wrong.”

The Critical Question

What happens if this output is wrong?

If the answer is “nothing much” → quick check.

If the answer is “serious consequences” → full verification.

This is the question to ask yourself every single time you use AI output in your work. What happens if this is wrong? If the answer is “nothing much” — it is a personal brainstorm, a draft for your own thinking — then a quick check is fine. If the answer is “serious consequences” — reputational damage, financial loss, regulatory exposure — then you need full verification. This is not about being paranoid. It is about being professional. You would not let a junior analyst present unchecked numbers to the board. Do not let an AI do it either.

Demo: Skills and Structured Prompts

Now let me show you something that takes your productivity to the next level. So far, every time you use Claude, you start from scratch. You describe what you want, you iterate, you get a good result. But what if you do the same type of task every week? A status report, a data analysis, a document review? You should not have to re-explain what you want every time. That is where skills and structured prompts come in.

What Is a Skill?

A skill is a reusable set of instructions that produces consistent output every time.

Write it once. Reuse it every week. Get the same quality without re-explaining.

Think of it as a template for your AI — not a document template with blanks to fill in, but an instruction set that tells the AI exactly what to produce, in what format, with what tone and structure.

A skill is a reusable set of instructions. Instead of telling Claude what you want from scratch every time, you write a detailed prompt once — specifying the sections, the format, the tone, the level of detail — and then you reuse it. Every time you run the skill, you get consistent, high-quality output without having to re-explain your preferences. Think of it as the difference between giving a new employee instructions every day versus writing them a standard operating procedure.

Example: Weekly Status Report Skill

Reusable prompt

You are a business analyst preparing a weekly status report.

Format: Start with a 2-sentence executive summary. Then three sections: 1. Key Accomplishments (bullet points, max 5) 2. Risks and Blockers (bullet points with severity: high/medium/low) 3. Next Week Priorities (numbered list, max 4)

Tone: Direct, factual, no filler. Write for a VP who reads 20 of these. Length: One page maximum. Always end with: “Decision needed:” followed by any open items requiring management input, or “None this week.”

Here is a concrete example. This is a weekly status report skill. It specifies exactly what Claude should produce: a two-sentence executive summary, three sections with specific formats, a direct tone written for a VP who reads twenty of these, a one-page maximum, and a closing section for decisions needed. You write this once. Then every week, you paste in your notes and run the skill. Claude produces a consistent, well-formatted status report in seconds. The quality is repeatable because the instructions are explicit.

The /critique Pattern: Three Independent Reviewers

Correctness & Completeness

Are there factual errors, logical gaps, or missing information? Are claims supported and assumptions justified?

Clarity & Persuasiveness

Is the structure logical? Is anything confusing, redundant, or buried? Could the main message be stated more directly?

Devil’s Advocate

What are the strongest counterarguments? Where is the reasoning weakest? What would a skeptic challenge?

Three parallel reviewers examine the same output from different angles, then synthesize findings. The result is dramatically more reliable than any single-pass review.

This is the critique pattern, and it is the most powerful quality technique in the course. Instead of reviewing the output yourself from one perspective, you ask the AI to spawn three independent reviewers — each with a different lens. One checks correctness and completeness: are the facts right, are there gaps, are assumptions justified? One checks clarity and persuasiveness: is the structure logical, is anything buried or confusing? And one plays devil’s advocate: what would a skeptic challenge, where is the reasoning weakest? The three reviews are synthesized and applied. The result is dramatically stronger than any single-pass output — and it takes about 30 seconds.

You have about fifteen minutes.

Tips for Building Your Workflow

What makes a good skill

  • Be explicit about format: sections, length, bullet vs. paragraph, headers
  • Specify tone: formal vs. conversational, direct vs. diplomatic
  • Define the audience: who reads this? What do they care about?
  • Include examples: “A good bullet point looks like this: …”
  • Add constraints: “Never exceed one page” or “Always include a recommendation”

Test it 2-3 times with different inputs. A good skill works consistently, not just once.

A few tips as you build. Be explicit about format — specify sections, length limits, whether you want bullets or paragraphs, what the headers should be. Specify the tone — formal or conversational, direct or diplomatic. Define the audience — who reads this and what do they care about? Include examples of what good output looks like. And add constraints — “never exceed one page” or “always include a recommendation.” Then test it two or three times with different inputs. A skill that only works once is not a skill — it should produce consistent quality across different inputs.
