How Data Moves Today

Now let’s step back and understand the landscape. What you just saw — an AI agent querying multiple systems and merging results on the fly — is one approach to data integration. But it is not the only one, and it is not always the right one. Let me walk you through how data moves in enterprises today so you can understand where this new approach fits.

This lecture segment should take about 12 minutes.

Four Patterns of Data Integration

APIs

  • Real-time, point-to-point
  • Each connection hand-coded
  • Breaks when vendors change endpoints

iPaaS

  • MuleSoft, Workato, Boomi
  • Pre-built connectors
  • Still needs mapping and maintenance

ETL / Warehouse

  • Extract, transform, load into Snowflake/BigQuery
  • Single source of truth
  • $500K–$5M and 6–18 months

CSV Export

  • The “just email me the file” approach
  • No governance, no audit trail
  • Surprisingly common

There are four main patterns for how data moves between systems in enterprises today. First, APIs — real-time, point-to-point connections. Each one is hand-coded, and they break when vendors change their endpoints. Second, integration platforms like MuleSoft, Workato, or Boomi — these offer pre-built connectors, which is better, but you still need to map fields and maintain the connections over time.

Third, the ETL and data warehouse approach — extract, transform, load into something like Snowflake or BigQuery. This gives you a single source of truth, which is the gold standard. But the cost is 500K to 5 million dollars and 6 to 18 months to build. And fourth — and I hesitate to even call this a “pattern” — the CSV export. The “just email me the file” approach. No governance, no audit trail. And it is surprisingly common. If you are honest with yourself, how much of your company’s cross-system data integration happens in Excel spreadsheets that get emailed around?

Ask the room: which of these four patterns does your company rely on most? I bet the answer is some combination of all four, depending on which systems and which teams are involved.

Cross-System Friction

Now let’s look at the unsexy but critical part of cross-system data: the friction. This is what makes merging hard, whether you are building a warehouse or using an AI agent. The technology changes; the friction does not.

Let me walk through the specific problems that make cross-system merging hard. Look at this table. Naming conventions: one system uses camelCase for column names, another uses snake_case. Same data, different labels — the agent has to know that “customerName” and “customer_name” refer to the same thing.

Date formats: one system stores dates as “2025-03-15” and another stores them as “March 15, 2025” — or even worse, as a text string in MM/DD/YYYY format. If you are doing date-based aggregations, this causes parse failures and wrong results.

But the hardest problem is customer identity. “General Electric” in Salesforce and “GE Industrial Solutions” in the Legacy CRM — these are the same company, but there is no common key linking them. No shared customer ID. Just a name that is spelled differently in every system. And it gets worse: “Honeywell International” and “Honeywell Safety Products” — are those the same entity? Or is one a subsidiary? That is a business judgment call, not a technical one.

XYZ has about 25 customers that appear under different names across its three CRMs. This is not a bug in our simulation. This is the normal state of enterprise data. Every company I have worked with has this problem. If you have grown through acquisition or have divisions with autonomy over their CRM, you have this problem.

The Fuzzy Matching Problem

How Does the Agent Resolve “General Electric” vs. “GE Industrial Solutions”?

  • Exact match fails — the strings are completely different
  • Fuzzy matching (Levenshtein distance, token-set ratio) catches ~80% of cases
  • Semantic matching (embeddings) catches more, but is slower and less transparent
  • Human review is still needed for ambiguous cases (is “Honeywell Safety Products” a subsidiary or a division?)

This is the unsexy core of data integration. No amount of AI eliminates the need to decide what your entities are and maintain a mapping.

So how does the agent resolve “General Electric” versus “GE Industrial Solutions”? Exact string matching fails completely — the strings are totally different. Fuzzy matching — techniques like Levenshtein distance and token-set ratio — catches about 80 percent of cases. It is looking for strings that are similar enough to be plausible matches. Semantic matching using embeddings can catch more, but it is slower and less transparent. And for ambiguous cases, you still need human review.

Here is the honest truth, and I want to underline the text at the bottom of this slide: this is the unsexy core of data integration. No amount of AI eliminates the need to decide what your entities are and maintain a mapping. Someone in your organization needs to define: is “Honeywell Safety Products” the same as “Honeywell International” for reporting purposes? That is a business decision. The AI can suggest matches, but a human needs to confirm them and maintain the mapping over time.

This is often where the excitement about AI data tools meets the reality of enterprise data management. The technology is impressive, but the hard work is still defining your master data. Keep this in mind as we move into the hands-on portion.

Before we wrap up, I want to name something important that the demos do not show. Before any AI agent can query your real systems — not the XYZ simulation, but your actual production systems — someone has to do a significant amount of pre-work.

You need to catalog the data: what tables exist, what do the columns mean? You need to document the schemas: data types, relationships, business rules. You need to provision API access: authentication, rate limits, permissions. And you need to classify PII: which fields contain personal data, and what are the retention rules?
