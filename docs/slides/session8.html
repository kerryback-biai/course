What Makes a Strong Pitch

Evaluation Dimensions

  • Specificity — a concrete use case, not “we’ll use AI for analytics”
  • Business case — who benefits, what decision gets faster, what’s the dollar impact
  • Data readiness — where does the data live, what’s the integration challenge
  • Governance — who validates answers, what are the guardrails
  • Day 1 action — what happens Monday morning, not “we’ll form a committee”

The best pitches are narrow and actionable. “We’ll deploy document AI on our 200 vendor contracts so procurement can answer compliance questions in seconds” beats “We’ll implement an enterprise AI strategy.”

Before you go into breakout rooms, let me remind you of the evaluation dimensions. These are from the rubric you used to self-check your strategy documents.

Specificity: a concrete use case with a named team, a named data source, and a named decision that gets better. Not “we will use AI for analytics.” That is a slogan, not a strategy.

Business case: who benefits, what decision gets faster, and ideally, what is the dollar impact? Even a rough estimate — “this saves our analysts twenty hours a week” — is better than nothing.

Data readiness: where does the data live, and what is the integration challenge? If you are proposing to query data across three systems with different naming conventions, say so. If you are proposing document AI on contracts that are scanned PDFs, acknowledge the ingestion challenge.

Governance: who validates the AI’s answers? What are the guardrails? We spent all of Session 6 on this. If your strategy does not address governance, it is not ready for production.

Day 1 action: what happens Monday morning? A specific person does a specific thing. Not “we will form a committee” or “we will explore options.” The best Day 1 actions I have seen are things like “I will email our VP of Sales with a one-page proposal and a request for thirty minutes” or “I will pull the top twenty vendor contracts and test them against Microsoft Copilot.”

Read the explainer at the bottom. “We will deploy document AI on our 200 vendor contracts so procurement can answer compliance questions in seconds” — that is narrow and actionable. “We will implement an enterprise AI strategy” — that is neither.

Let me pull together the five big ideas from this entire course. These are the things I want you to carry with you long after the details fade.

First, AI is a personal productivity multiplier. Charts, documents, analyses, automated workflows — all available right now, no IT approval needed. You experienced this in Sessions 1 and 2. The tools you used on day one are tools you can use tomorrow morning.

Second, plain English equals enterprise data answers. Third, simple but not easy. In Session 4, you saw that a working agent is about fifty lines of code. The technology is simpler than most people expect. You learned database AI in Sessions 3 through 6, predictive modeling in Session 7, and document AI rounds out the picture. Together they cover the full spectrum of data-driven decisions, and the most powerful strategies we heard today combined multiple capabilities.

Fifth, the critical skill. The human skill that matters most is not building AI tools. It is asking the right questions, iterating on output, and verifying the answers. The maker/checker mindset is the throughline of this entire course. If you ask a bad question, you get a bad answer faster. If you do not verify, you propagate errors at scale. The maker/checker framework is not optional — it is essential.

AI as Thinking Partner

The real power isn’t asking what happened. It’s asking what should we do — and pushing back on the answer.

AI anchors on whatever framing you provide. The human role is challenging assumptions, not outsourcing judgment.

Here is the shift. Everything we have done so far has been asking “what happened” — what were sales last quarter, what does the contract say, what did the board approve. Those are retrieval questions. The AI goes and gets information. But the real power is asking “what should we do” — and then pushing back on the answer.

When you ask a strategic question, the AI does not just retrieve data. It synthesizes across multiple sources, identifies patterns, and offers recommendations. That is powerful. But here is the critical caveat, and I want you to remember this. AI anchors on whatever framing you provide. If you ask “should we expand into the Southeast?” the AI will tend to find evidence that supports expansion — because that is how you framed the question. The human role is challenging assumptions, not outsourcing judgment. You push back. You reframe. You ask “what if our supply chain cannot handle the volume?” and see how the analysis changes.

This is where AI goes from being a reporting tool to being a thinking partner. But only if you treat it like a colleague you argue with, not an oracle you defer to.

In Turn 3, the user asked the agent to push back on its own risk assessment. The 30-Day Sprint

Week 1

Pick & Prep

Choose one use case. Identify the data source. Get stakeholder buy-in.

Week 2

Build & Test

Connect the data. Build the prototype. Test with real questions from real users.

Week 3

Validate

Compare AI answers to known results. Document accuracy. Identify failure modes.

Week 4

Decide

Go/no-go for expanded pilot. Present findings. Define Phase 2 scope.

If you are going to run that thirty-day sprint, here is a week-by-week framework. Week 1: pick one use case, identify the data source, get stakeholder buy-in. The most important thing in Week 1 is the stakeholder conversation. If you do not have a business owner who cares about the outcome, the pilot will stall.

Week 2: connect the data, build the prototype, and start testing with real questions from real users. Not hypothetical questions — real questions that people actually need answered. If you are doing document AI, load the actual contracts or policies. If you are doing database AI, connect to the actual data source.

Week 3: validate. Compare AI answers to known results. Document accuracy and identify failure modes. This is the Session 6 maker/checker framework applied to your pilot. How often is the AI right? How often is it wrong? What kinds of questions does it handle well, and where does it break down?

Week 4: decide. Go or no-go for an expanded pilot. Present your findings to stakeholders. Define the Phase 2 scope based on what you learned. The decision is not “should we use AI” — the decision is “should we expand this specific use case to more users, more data, or more systems.”

This framework is deliberately simple. Complexity is the enemy of execution. Keep it tight, keep it moving, and learn as you go.
