Two Kinds of AI for Business

Database AI (Sessions 1–6)

  • Queries structured data in rows and columns
  • Writes SQL, runs it, returns results
  • Best for: numbers, trends, KPIs, comparisons
  • Source of truth: your transactional systems

Document AI (Today)

  • Queries unstructured text in PDFs, Word docs, policies
  • Retrieves relevant passages, synthesizes an answer with citations
  • Best for: policies, contracts, procedures, meeting notes
  • Source of truth: your document repositories

Most real business questions need both. “What’s our contractual commitment to GE, and how does their actual spending compare?” requires document AI for the contract and database AI for the spending data.

Let me frame what we are adding today. On the left is everything you already know — database AI. It queries structured data in rows and columns, writes SQL, runs it, and returns numbers, charts, and narratives. That is Sessions 1 through 6 in a nutshell. On the right is document AI, which is today’s focus. Instead of structured data, it works with unstructured text — PDFs, Word documents, policies, contracts, meeting notes. The key distinction is the source of truth. Database AI pulls from your transactional systems — your CRM, your ERP, your financial systems. The data types are fundamentally different, and the retrieval mechanisms are fundamentally different. But here is the important point at the bottom of the slide. Most real business questions need both. Read that example in the explainer: “What is our contractual commitment to GE, and how does their actual spending compare?” The contract terms live in a PDF. The actual spending lives in a database. You need document AI for the first part and database AI for the second. We are going to see exactly that kind of combined question in our hands-on today.

How RAG Works

1

Chunk

Documents split into passages (paragraphs, sections, or clauses)

2

Embed

Each chunk converted to a numerical vector by an embedding model

3

Store

Vectors stored in a vector database for fast similarity search

4

Query

User’s question embedded the same way; top matching chunks retrieved

5

Generate

LLM receives question + retrieved chunks and produces a cited answer

RAG = Retrieval-Augmented Generation. The LLM only sees what the retrieval step finds. If retrieval fails, the answer fails — no matter how smart the model is.

RAG stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Let me walk you through the five steps. You do not need to memorize these, but understanding the pipeline will help you evaluate vendors and diagnose problems.

Step 1: Chunk. Your documents are split into passages — paragraphs, sections, or clauses depending on the document type. This is where the Atherton chunking failure came from. If you split a contract clause in the middle of a sentence, you have already lost. Step 2: Embed. Each chunk is converted into a numerical vector by an embedding model. Think of this as translating text into a position in a high-dimensional space where similar meanings end up near each other. “Remote work policy” and “work from home guidelines” would end up close together even though they use different words. Step 3: Store. Those vectors go into a specialized database — a vector database — that is optimized for finding similar vectors quickly. Step 4: Query. When a user asks a question, that question goes through the same embedding process, and the vector database finds the stored chunks that are closest in meaning to the question. Step 5: Generate. The LLM receives the user’s question plus the top matching chunks as context, and it produces an answer using only that retrieved context, with citations pointing back to the source documents.

Here is the critical insight, and I want you to underline this in your mental model. Read the explainer at the bottom. The LLM only sees what the retrieval step finds. If retrieval fails, the answer fails — no matter how smart the model is. You could have the most advanced language model in the world, and if step 4 hands it the wrong chunks, the answer will be wrong. RAG quality is retrieval quality. That is why chunking strategy, which we will discuss later, is the single highest-leverage decision in any document AI deployment.

The Key Insight

Let me make this point as starkly as possible. This is the single most important thing to understand about document AI.

[TIMING: Pause on this divider for just a beat, then advance to the statement slide. The dramatic pause is intentional.]

The LLM doesn’t read your documents.

It reads whatever the retrieval system hands it.

RAG quality is retrieval quality. If the retrieval step fails, the answer fails — no matter how smart the model is.

I want you to internalize this. The LLM does not read your documents. It reads whatever the retrieval system hands it. This means that when you are evaluating a document AI vendor, or scoping a build, the question is not “which LLM are you using?” The question is “how good is your retrieval?” How do you chunk? How do you embed? How do you handle documents that span multiple topics? How do you handle updates? The retrieval pipeline is where the quality lives or dies.

Think back to Atherton. Their LLM was fine. Their retrieval was broken. Contract clauses split mid-sentence, so the model got half a clause and produced a confident, dangerously incomplete answer. The model did exactly what it was supposed to do — it answered based on the context it was given. The problem was upstream, in the chunking.

When RAG Fails

Retrieval Misses

  • Question phrased differently than source text
  • Relevant chunk not in top results
  • Result: correct answer exists but system can’t find it

Hallucinated Citations

  • LLM generates plausible-sounding references
  • Citation points to wrong section or nonexistent page
  • Result: answer looks authoritative but is wrong

Stale Documents

  • Policy was updated but old version still indexed
  • System answers from superseded document
  • Result: confidently delivers outdated information

Live demo: Watch what happens when a contract clause is split across two chunks. The system retrieves half the clause and gives a dangerously incomplete answer.

Now let me show you the failure modes. Three categories, and each one maps back to the Atherton case we discussed at the opening.

First, retrieval misses. The answer exists in the document collection, but the retrieval step does not find it. This happens when the question is phrased differently than the source text, or when the relevant chunk does not rank high enough to make the top results. The answer is there — the system just cannot find it. Some of you may have seen this in Round 2 when the system found one document but missed the other.

Second, hallucinated citations. This is the most dangerous failure mode. The LLM generates a plausible-sounding reference — it cites a specific page number, quotes what looks like a passage — but when you go check, that passage does not actually say what the AI claims, or it does not exist at all. The answer looks authoritative, which makes it harder to catch. This is why we said in Session 6: always check the cited passage, not just the summary.

Third, stale documents. A policy was updated, but the old version is still in the index. The system answers from the superseded document and confidently delivers outdated information. This was one of Atherton’s failures, and it is incredibly common in real deployments because document management is one of those things nobody wants to own.

Context Window Limits

Even the best models have a ceiling

If your question requires context from 50 pages of contracts, the model can only see what fits in its context window. Retrieval quality matters because you can’t just dump everything in. Chunking strategy determines what the model gets to see.

One more technical constraint that is important for your mental model. Even the best language models have a context window — a limit on how much text they can process at once. Today’s frontier models can handle very long contexts, but there is still a ceiling. If your question requires context from fifty pages of contracts, the model cannot see all fifty pages at once. The retrieval step has to select which passages to include.

This is why you cannot just dump all your documents into the LLM and skip the retrieval step. Some people ask, “Why not just send the entire contract to the model?” For a single short document, you can. But for a collection of hundreds of documents, or even for a single hundred-page contract, you need retrieval to select the relevant passages. Chunking strategy determines what the model gets to see, which means chunking strategy determines the quality of the answer. We keep coming back to the same point: retrieval quality is everything.

Deploying RAG: Key Decisions

1

Document Ingestion

PDF parsing, OCR for scans, handling tables and images

2

Chunking Strategy

By section for policies, by clause for contracts, by paragraph for memos

3

Freshness

Re-index on update, version tracking, retire superseded docs

4

Access Control

Document-level permissions must carry over — who can see what

Chunking is the single highest-leverage decision. Bad chunking = bad retrieval = bad answers, regardless of model quality.

The deployment framework from Session 5 — cloud versus on-premise, build versus buy — still applies here, so we are not going to repeat those general principles. What is new for document AI are these four specific decisions.

First, document ingestion. How do documents get into the system? You need PDF parsing that handles different formats. You need OCR for scanned documents. You need to handle tables and images, which are notoriously difficult for text extraction. If your company has contracts that are scanned PDFs of faxes from 2003, that is an ingestion challenge you need to plan for.

Second, chunking strategy, and I have been hammering this point because it is the single highest-leverage decision. The right chunking strategy depends on the document type. Policies should be chunked by section. Contracts should be chunked by clause — you never want to split a clause across two chunks, which is exactly what happened at Atherton. Memos and meeting notes should be chunked by paragraph or topic. One size does not fit all, and the vendor that tells you otherwise is selling you something.

Third, freshness. When a policy is updated, the old version must be replaced, not duplicated. You need re-indexing on update, version tracking so you know which version the system is answering from, and a process to retire superseded documents. Atherton’s stale document problem came from having no freshness management at all. The old policy and the new policy were both in the index, and the system returned whichever the retrieval step happened to find first.

Fourth, access control. The RAG system must enforce the same document permissions as your file server. If board minutes are restricted to senior leadership, the RAG system cannot show those passages to everyone. If HR policies are scoped by division, the system needs to respect those boundaries. Atherton’s access control gap — junior staff seeing board-level compensation data — is the cautionary tale here.

Build vs. Buy

Turnkey Platforms Custom Build
Examples Glean, Guru, Microsoft Copilot for M365 LangChain + vector DB + your infra
Setup time Days to weeks Weeks to months
Customization Limited — works with standard doc formats Full control over chunking, retrieval, prompts
Access control Built-in (inherits from source system) You build it
Cost model Per-user subscription Infrastructure + engineering time
Best for Broad knowledge base across standard tools Domain-specific docs, regulatory, proprietary formats

Most companies start with a turnkey platform for general knowledge and build custom RAG only where the domain requires it (legal, compliance, engineering specs).

For document AI specifically, the build-versus-buy landscape looks like this. On the left, turnkey platforms — Glean, Guru, Microsoft Copilot for M365. These handle ingestion and permissions out of the box. They connect to your existing document sources — SharePoint, Google Drive, Confluence — and they inherit the access controls from those systems. Setup time is days to weeks, and the cost model is typically per-user subscription. The tradeoff is limited customization. You get their chunking strategy, their retrieval approach, their prompts.

On the right, custom builds using tools like LangChain with a vector database on your own infrastructure. This gives you full control over chunking, retrieval, and prompts. You can implement domain-specific chunking — by clause for contracts, by regulation for compliance documents. But you build the access control yourself, and the setup time is weeks to months rather than days.

The practical pattern I see most often is what the explainer describes. Companies start with a turnkey platform for general knowledge — the employee handbook, standard policies, IT documentation. Then they build custom RAG only where the domain demands it — legal contracts with specific clause structures, regulatory filings with complex cross-references, engineering specifications with tables and diagrams that standard chunking cannot handle.

For your capstone strategies, think about where your company falls on this spectrum. Do you have standard documents that a turnkey platform could handle? Or do you have domain-specific documents that require custom chunking and retrieval?
