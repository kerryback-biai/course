The “Why Now”

In February 2026, AI agents wiped $2 trillion from software company valuations.

So why does this matter right now? Let me set the context. In February 2026, something remarkable happened. AI agents — software that can understand plain English and take action — triggered the largest repricing in software history. Two trillion dollars in market value disappeared in a matter of weeks. The market sent a very clear signal: AI can replace large categories of enterprise software. Whether or not you agree with the market’s verdict, the technology behind it is real, and it is moving fast.

The SAASpocalypse

“The AI agent revolution is wreaking havoc on traditional SaaS valuations. Investors are betting that intelligent agents will replace the click-heavy workflows that enterprise software companies have spent decades building.”

— CNBC, “Software stocks face worst rout in a decade as AI agents threaten business models,” February 2026

The press called it the SAASpocalypse. CNBC, TechCrunch, and every major financial outlet covered it. The thesis was simple: if an AI agent can do what a human does inside Salesforce, or SAP, or ServiceNow — querying data, generating reports, automating workflows — then why pay per-seat license fees for the software itself? The market decided that the answer, for many categories, is: you will not need to.

The Damage

−31% Salesforce

−32% Adobe

−35% Atlassian

Here are some of the numbers. Salesforce, the world’s largest CRM company, lost 31 percent of its value. Adobe, which dominates creative software, dropped 32 percent. Atlassian, the maker of Jira and Confluence — tools used by virtually every software team — fell 35 percent. These are not small companies. These are pillars of the enterprise software industry, and the market is telling them their moats may be thinner than they thought.

What AI Is Replacing

The Interface Layer

  • Click-heavy workflows
  • Form-filling and navigation
  • Manual data entry across screens
  • “Training” users on software

Workflow Automation

  • Rule-based routing
  • Report scheduling
  • Template-driven outputs
  • Repetitive analysis tasks

Per-Seat Economics

  • $150-300/user/month SaaS fees
  • Shelfware (paid but unused)
  • Feature bloat nobody asked for
  • Integration tax between tools

So what exactly is AI replacing? Three things. First, the interface layer — all those clicks, forms, and navigation that enterprise software forces you through. If you can just say what you want in English, the UI becomes unnecessary. Second, workflow automation — rules-based routing, report scheduling, template-driven outputs. AI handles these natively. Third, the per-seat economics model itself. Companies pay 150 to 300 dollars per user per month for SaaS tools, and much of that capacity goes unused. AI collapses the cost structure.

What Survives

Data

The systems of record. The databases. The institutional knowledge embedded in your CRM, ERP, and HR systems.

AI does not replace your data — it makes it accessible.

But here is what does not get replaced: data. Your CRM still holds your customer relationships. Your ERP still tracks your supply chain. Your HR system still manages your people. AI does not replace those systems of record. What it does is make the data inside them dramatically more accessible. Instead of needing a BI team to build a dashboard, you ask a question in English. That is the shift — and it is why we spend the second half of this course on enterprise data.

How AI + Code + Data Work Together

Now let me explain something important about how these systems actually work. Understanding this architecture will help you make sense of everything we cover in this course — from personal productivity today to enterprise deployment in later sessions.

Three Moving Pieces

The AI Brain

  • The large language model (LLM)
  • Always in the cloud — hosted by Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, etc.
  • Understands your question
  • Generates text, code, and plans
  • Has no permanent memory

The Data

  • Could be on your computer (spreadsheets, local files)
  • Could be in SaaS provider storage (Salesforce, Workday, NetSuite)
  • Could be on company-managed servers (on-prem or cloud)
  • Where data lives determines security requirements

Code Execution

  • Something has to run the Python, SQL, or scripts the AI writes
  • Could run on the LLM provider’s hardware (Claude.ai sandbox)
  • Could run on company servers (Docker, on-prem)
  • Could run on your computer (Claude Desktop, terminal)

There are three moving pieces in every AI system, and understanding where each one lives is the key to understanding the whole course. First, the AI brain — the large language model. This is always in the cloud, hosted by the provider. It understands your question, generates text and code, but has no permanent memory. Second, the data. This could be on your own computer — a spreadsheet, a PDF. It could be in SaaS provider storage — your Salesforce CRM, your Workday HR system. Or it could be on company-managed servers. Where your data lives determines the security requirements. Third, code execution. When the AI writes Python or SQL to analyze data, something has to actually run that code. It could run on the LLM provider’s hardware — that is what happens when you use Claude.ai’s sandbox. It could run on your company’s servers. Or it could run on your own computer. Each combination has different implications for security, cost, and capability.

Exercise 3: Draft an Executive Summary

Instructions

Exercise three is about written documents. I have provided a two-page quarterly briefing document. Paste it into Claude and ask for a one-page executive summary for the CEO. The Maker/Checker Preview

The Maker

  • AI generates the first draft
  • Fast, tireless, format-flexible
  • No domain expertise
  • No sense of stakes or audience
  • Will confidently produce wrong answers

The Checker

  • You verify, refine, and approve
  • Domain knowledge and judgment
  • Understands the audience
  • Knows what “wrong” costs
  • The irreplaceable human role

Before we wrap up, I want to plant a seed that will grow throughout this entire course. Everything AI produces needs verification. Everything. The question is not whether to check — it is how much to check. And the answer depends on the stakes. This is the maker/checker framework. AI is the maker — fast, tireless, format-flexible, but with no domain expertise and no sense of what getting it wrong would cost. You are the checker — you bring judgment, domain knowledge, and an understanding of your audience. We will formalize this framework next session.

The Spectrum of Trust

🟢 Low Stakes Personal notes, brainstorming, exploration

🟡 Medium Stakes Team decks, internal reports, summaries

🔴 High Stakes Board decks, regulatory filings, public docs

🎯 Your Judgment Match verification effort to consequences

Think of it as a spectrum. Green: low stakes — personal notes, brainstorming, quick explorations. A fast sanity check is enough. Yellow: medium stakes — team presentations, internal reports, meeting summaries. You need to verify numbers and check that the framing is accurate. Red: high stakes — board decks, regulatory filings, anything public-facing. Every claim, every number, every source needs independent verification. And the skill you are developing is judgment — knowing where each output falls on this spectrum and calibrating your verification effort accordingly. Next session, we formalize this into a framework you can apply to every AI interaction.
