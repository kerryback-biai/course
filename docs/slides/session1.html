A Familiar Story

The Email Chain

Monday 9:14 AM — CEO to VP Sales: “Which customers buy from multiple divisions? I want the list by Wednesday.”

Monday 11:02 AM — VP Sales to BI Team: “Need a cross-division customer overlap report. CEO priority.”

Monday 3:30 PM — BI Team: “We can start next sprint. ETA: 3 weeks. We need to join Salesforce, Legacy CRM, and HubSpot — different schemas, no common key.”

Monday 3:45 PM — CEO: “Three weeks?”

Read through this email chain with the class. Monday morning, 9:14 AM — the CEO fires off a simple question: which of our customers buy from multiple divisions? Seems straightforward, right? By 11 AM it has landed on the BI team’s desk as a priority request. And by 3:30 PM, the BI team responds: three weeks. They need to join Salesforce, the Legacy CRM, and HubSpot — different schemas, no common key. The CEO’s reaction at 3:45 says it all: three weeks?

Now, ask the room: has this happened to you? Not this exact scenario, but the pattern — a senior leader asks what feels like a simple question, and the answer is weeks away because the data lives in different systems that were never designed to talk to each other. I want to hear a couple of examples. Let two or three people share briefly before moving on.

The reason I am showing you this is that it sets up the core promise of what we are about to see. What if this question did not take three weeks? What if it took thirty seconds?

Dashboard vs. Chat

Traditional Dashboard

  • Pre-built by analysts weeks ago
  • Fixed filters and dimensions
  • Can’t answer questions it wasn’t designed for
  • New question = new ticket = new sprint

AI Chat Interface

  • Ask any question in plain English
  • Agent writes SQL, runs it, returns results
  • Generates charts and narrative on the fly
  • Follow-up questions in seconds

Now let’s connect what you just saw back to the broader picture. On the left, you have the traditional dashboard — pre-built by analysts weeks ago, with fixed filters and fixed dimensions. It can only answer the questions it was designed for. If you have a new question, that is a new ticket, a new sprint, a new wait.

On the right, the AI chat interface. You ask any question in plain English. The agent writes SQL, runs it, returns results. It generates charts and narratives on the fly. And follow-up questions take seconds, not weeks. The key difference is not speed — it is flexibility. The dashboard answers the questions someone anticipated. The chat interface answers the questions you actually have, right now, in the moment.

I want to be clear: dashboards are not going away. They are still the right tool for operational monitoring — metrics you check every day. But for the long tail of ad hoc questions that never justified building a dashboard? That is where this technology changes everything.

The Key Question

If anyone in your company could query data in plain English — no SQL, no tickets, no dashboards — what decisions get made faster? What questions finally get asked?

This is the key question I want you to sit with. If anyone in your company — not just the analysts, not just the people who know SQL — could query data in plain English, what changes? Think about it in two dimensions.

First, what decisions get made faster? The decisions that are already being made, but slowly, because someone has to pull the data, build the report, and route it through the right people. Those decisions get compressed from days or weeks to minutes.

But the second dimension is more interesting: what questions finally get asked? There are questions in every organization that nobody bothers asking because the cost of getting the answer is too high. It would take three weeks and a dedicated analyst, so people just go with their gut. When the cost of asking drops to zero, those questions start getting asked. And that is where the real value is.

Ask the room: can anyone give me an example of a question at your company that nobody asks because it is too hard to get the data? Let two or three people respond. This discussion is important — it makes the abstract concrete for each participant.

Meet Meridian Corp

Your Simulation Environment

  • Meridian Corp — a $500M industrial distributor
  • 3 divisions: Industrial (~$250M), Energy (~$150M), Safety (~$100M)
  • 10 enterprise systems — CRMs, ERP, HRIS, ticketing, and more
  • 41 database tables across all systems
  • 26,000+ rows of realistic, interconnected data spanning 3 years

Before you start querying, let me tell you about the company you are going to be working with. Meridian Corp is a fictional 500-million-dollar industrial distributor. It has three operating divisions: Industrial at about 250 million, Energy at about 150 million, and Safety at about 100 million. The company runs on ten enterprise systems — CRMs, ERP, HRIS, ticketing, and more — with 41 database tables and over 26,000 rows of realistic, interconnected data spanning three years.

This is not a toy dataset. It has the same kind of cross-system friction you deal with in real life: different naming conventions, different date formats, customers that appear under different names in different systems. We built it to feel real because the lessons only land if the data feels real.

Meridian’s Systems at a Glance

Industrial Division (~$250M)

  • Salesforce CRM
  • NetSuite Finance
  • SAP Operations

Energy Division (~$150M)

  • Legacy CRM (custom-built)
  • QuickBooks Finance
  • Oracle SCM

Safety Division (~$100M)

  • HubSpot CRM
  • Shared NetSuite Finance

Corporate HQ

  • Workday HR (all divisions)
  • NetSuite Consolidation
  • Zendesk Support

Three divisions. Three CRMs. Ten enterprise systems. This is the cross-system reality most enterprises face.

Take a moment to look at this layout. Three divisions, each with their own CRM and financial systems. Industrial uses Salesforce and NetSuite. Energy uses a legacy custom-built CRM and QuickBooks. Safety uses HubSpot and shares NetSuite with Industrial. Then corporate has Workday for HR across all divisions, NetSuite for consolidation, and Zendesk for support.

Notice the pattern: three CRMs. Three different data models. Three different ways of storing customer information. This is not bad IT management — this is what happens when companies grow through acquisition, when divisions have autonomy, when different teams picked the best tool for their needs at different times. It is the normal state of enterprise data, and it is exactly why that CEO’s question about cross-division customers took three weeks.

For today, you will be querying individual systems. Next session, we tackle the hard problem of querying across them.

Key Takeaway

Let me leave you with the key takeaway from today’s session.

The bottleneck was never the data.

It was access to the data.

AI agents don’t replace your analysts — they give everyone else the ability to ask questions without waiting in line.

The bottleneck was never the data. Your company has plenty of data — in Salesforce, in NetSuite, in Workday, in a dozen other systems. The bottleneck was access to the data. It was the three-week wait for a custom report. It was the dashboard that could not answer the question you actually had. It was the analyst who was busy with someone else’s priority.

AI agents do not replace your analysts. Your analysts are still the people who understand the business context, who know what questions to ask, who can verify whether an answer makes sense. What AI agents do is give everyone else the ability to ask questions without waiting in line. That is the shift. The cost of asking a question drops to zero, and when that happens, the questions start flowing.
